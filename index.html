About

I am an applied economist whose research sits at the intersection of political economy, public policy, and behavioural science. My work focuses on understanding corruption — its causes, its social-norm underpinnings, and how institutions can be designed to resist it.

My PhD dissertation, completed at the London School of Economics, examined "the interplay between corruption and social norms." I currently work on the Horizon Europe project BridgeGap at LUISS Guido Carli, Rome, and collaborate with the UN DESA, the World Bank, Transparency International, and the WHO's Lancet Commission on Corruption in Healthcare.

My research has been published in Governance, BMJ, Social Science and Medicine, BMJ Open, and Cambridge University Press, and has received media coverage from The Guardian and the BBC.

Research

Three interconnected streams of inquiry

01

Experimental Research in Governance

Behavioural experiments and lab-in-the-field designs studying social norms, corrupt reciprocity, and favouritism in allocation decisions.

02

Public Procurement & Integrity

Empirical analysis of favouritism in public procurement, supply-chain corruption, and the effectiveness of administrative anti-corruption interventions.

03

Conflicts of Interest & Lobbying

Mapping industry funding of patient organisations, conflicts of interest in clinical assessment, and the transparency of political finance in Europe.
