Research / III.

Experimental Research in Governance & Social Norms

This strand uses behavioural experiments to study how social norms, inequality, and institutional context shape individual decisions to engage in corruption — with direct implications for anti-corruption policy design.

Social norms and petty corruption

Standard economic models treat corruption as a rational response to low detection risk and high rewards. A growing body of evidence suggests this is incomplete: people also respond to perceived social norms — what they believe others do and consider acceptable. My work investigates how normative expectations interact with institutional signals to produce or suppress corrupt behaviour, with a focus on informal payments in healthcare settings.

This work draws on multilevel analysis of cross-national survey data and experimental methods to identify the independent effect of norms, net of material incentives. It was published in Governance (2024) and provides a foundation for behavioural approaches to anti-corruption policy.

Bribery, inequality, and favouritism — experimental evidence

My job market paper uses a lab-in-the-field experiment — an allocation game administered across varying institutional contexts — to test how economic inequality and perceptions of fairness shape the willingness to offer and accept bribes, and to exercise favouritism in resource allocation. The design allows me to disentangle strategic reciprocity from norm-driven behaviour, contributing to both the theory of corruption and the evidence base for institutional design.

Corrupt reciprocity — lab-in-the-field

In collaboration with Mylene Lagarde (LSE), I am conducting a lab-in-the-field experiment examining the behavioural mechanisms behind corrupt reciprocity — the tendency to favour those who have offered or are expected to offer bribes. The study examines how framing, group identity, and institutional context modulate these effects, and whether commonly proposed behavioural nudges can attenuate them.

Survey methodology for sensitive topics

Measuring corruption through surveys is methodologically challenging: respondents are reluctant to report illegal or socially undesirable behaviour. With Mylene Lagarde, I have examined how question framing affects reported rates of informal payment in healthcare, providing evidence on best practice for corruption surveys. This work was published in Social Science and Medicine (2025).
